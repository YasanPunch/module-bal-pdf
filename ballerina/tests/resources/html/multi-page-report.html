1. Executive Summary

The cloud infrastructure landscape in 2025 is defined by three converging forces: the maturation of platform engineering as a discipline, the rapid adoption of AI-driven operations, and an industry-wide reckoning with cloud cost management. This report, our third annual edition, draws on survey responses from 2,847 engineering leaders across 42 countries and direct analysis of over 15,000 production deployments.

Key Finding
Cloud spending grew 28% year-over-year, but 67% of organizations report that at least 30% of their cloud spend is wasted on idle or over-provisioned resources. The gap between investment and efficiency has never been wider.

Our analysis reveals that organizations successfully navigating this landscape share common characteristics: they treat infrastructure as a product, invest heavily in developer experience, and apply data-driven approaches to capacity planning. Perhaps most importantly, they have moved beyond the "lift and shift" mentality that defined the first wave of cloud adoption and are now designing cloud-native architectures that take full advantage of the platform's capabilities.

The emergence of platform engineering as a formalized discipline represents the single most significant organizational shift since the DevOps movement. Where DevOps blurred the line between development and operations, platform engineering creates a dedicated team responsible for building an Internal Developer Platform (IDP) — a curated set of tools, services, and workflows that abstract away infrastructure complexity and enable developers to self-serve. Our data shows that organizations with mature platform engineering teams deploy 4.3x more frequently and experience 60% fewer production incidents than those without.

2. Cloud Adoption and Spending

2.1 Market Size and Growth

Global cloud infrastructure spending reached $312 billion in 2024, a 28% increase from the previous year. While this growth rate represents a slight deceleration from the 32% growth seen in 2023, it reflects the sheer scale of the market rather than any slowdown in adoption. Enterprise cloud migration projects that were paused during the 2023 economic uncertainty have resumed, contributing to a backlog of modernization work that is now being executed.

The most notable trend in market dynamics is the accelerated growth of second-tier providers, particularly Oracle Cloud, which has leveraged its database heritage and aggressive pricing to win large enterprise workloads. Google Cloud's strong growth reflects its investments in AI/ML infrastructure, which has become a critical differentiator as organizations race to deploy generative AI applications.

2.2 Multi-Cloud and Hybrid Strategies

Multi-cloud adoption continues to increase, though the motivations have evolved. In previous years, organizations cited vendor lock-in avoidance as the primary driver. In our 2025 survey, the top motivation has shifted to "best-of-breed service selection" — teams want to use the best service for each workload regardless of provider. This represents a maturation in cloud strategy: rather than running the same workload across multiple clouds for redundancy, organizations are intentionally placing workloads where they perform best.

"We stopped trying to be cloud-agnostic. Instead, we're intentionally multi-cloud: analytics on BigQuery, compute on AWS, and AI training on Azure. The abstraction layer is our platform, not a lowest-common-denominator cloud API." — VP of Engineering, Fortune 500 retailer

However, multi-cloud strategies bring their own challenges. Our data reveals that organizations running workloads across three or more cloud providers spend an average of 23% more on infrastructure management overhead compared to single-cloud organizations. This "multi-cloud tax" includes the cost of maintaining expertise across platforms, managing cross-cloud networking, and operating heterogeneous monitoring and security stacks.

2.3 The Cost Management Crisis

Cloud cost management — or the lack thereof — has reached a critical inflection point. Despite the proliferation of FinOps practices and tools, our survey reveals persistent challenges:

  • 67% of organizations estimate that 30% or more of their cloud spend is wasted
  • Only 29% of organizations have a dedicated FinOps team or practice
  • 84% of engineering teams say they do not have visibility into the cost of their services at the team level
  • The average organization takes 45 days to detect and remediate significant cost anomalies
  • 52% of organizations exceeded their annual cloud budget by more than 20%

These findings paint a picture of an industry that has successfully adopted cloud technology but has not yet mastered the discipline of managing it efficiently. The root cause, in our assessment, is organizational rather than technical: cost management is treated as a finance function rather than an engineering discipline, and individual development teams lack both the visibility and the incentive to optimize their consumption.

3. Platform Engineering

3.1 The Rise of the Internal Developer Platform

Platform engineering has emerged as the dominant approach to managing infrastructure complexity. Rather than expecting every development team to become infrastructure experts, organizations are building dedicated platform teams that create a curated developer experience — what the industry calls an Internal Developer Platform (IDP).

Our survey data reveals the rapid adoption of this approach:

  1. 72% of organizations with more than 500 engineers now have a dedicated platform engineering team, up from 48% in 2023
  2. The median platform team size is 8 engineers for organizations with 500-2,000 total engineers
  3. Organizations with mature platform teams report 40% faster onboarding for new developers
  4. Developer satisfaction scores are 35% higher in organizations with well-designed IDPs
  5. The most common IDP components are: service catalogs (89%), automated environments (84%), deployment pipelines (82%), and observability dashboards (78%)

3.2 Measuring Platform Success

The metrics used to evaluate platform engineering effectiveness have matured significantly. Leading organizations track a combination of outcome metrics (deployment frequency, lead time for changes) and experience metrics (developer satisfaction, time to first deployment). The most sophisticated teams have adopted platform-as-a-product thinking, conducting user research with their internal developers and maintaining product roadmaps for their platform capabilities.

Key Finding
Organizations that treat their internal platform as a product (with dedicated product management, user research, and roadmaps) achieve 2.8x higher developer satisfaction and 1.7x faster feature delivery compared to those that treat it as an infrastructure project.

The tension between standardization and flexibility remains the central challenge for platform teams. Too much standardization stifles innovation and forces teams to work around the platform; too little standardization recreates the complexity the platform was designed to eliminate. The most successful platforms resolve this tension through "golden paths" — recommended, well-supported approaches that teams can follow for common scenarios, while preserving the ability to diverge when business needs require it.

4. Container Orchestration and Kubernetes

4.1 Kubernetes Adoption

Kubernetes has solidified its position as the default container orchestration platform, with 83% of organizations now running production workloads on Kubernetes. However, the operational complexity of Kubernetes has driven a significant shift toward managed offerings: 71% of Kubernetes users rely on managed services (EKS, GKE, AKS) rather than self-managed clusters.

The most significant development in the Kubernetes ecosystem this year is the graduation of the Gateway API to general availability. This represents the culmination of years of community effort to create a more expressive, extensible, and role-oriented approach to traffic management, replacing the aging Ingress API. Organizations that have migrated report significant reductions in configuration complexity and improved security through better separation of concerns between platform operators and application developers.

4.2 The Serverless Convergence

The boundary between containers and serverless is blurring. Platforms like AWS Fargate, Google Cloud Run, and Azure Container Apps offer a serverless experience for containerized workloads — no cluster management, automatic scaling, and pay-per-use pricing. Our survey shows that 41% of organizations now describe their primary compute strategy as "serverless containers," up from 23% in 2023.

This convergence is resolving one of the long-standing debates in cloud architecture: whether to invest in Kubernetes expertise or serverless simplicity. The answer, increasingly, is both — organizations use Kubernetes for complex, stateful workloads requiring fine-grained control, and serverless containers for the growing class of stateless, event-driven services that benefit from simplified operations.

5. AI and Machine Learning Infrastructure

5.1 The GPU Shortage and Its Implications

The demand for GPU compute has fundamentally altered cloud infrastructure planning. High-end GPUs (NVIDIA H100, A100) remain supply-constrained, with wait times of 3-6 months for new allocations at major cloud providers. This scarcity has driven several market responses:

  • GPU-as-a-Service startups have raised over $4 billion in funding, offering specialized GPU cloud infrastructure at competitive prices
  • Organizations are investing in model optimization techniques (quantization, distillation, pruning) to reduce GPU requirements by 40-60%
  • Alternative accelerators — Google TPUs, AWS Inferentia/Trainium, and AMD MI300 — are gaining traction as viable alternatives to NVIDIA's dominance
  • The emergence of inference-optimized hardware is creating a new market segment distinct from training infrastructure

5.2 MLOps and LLMOps

The operational practices around machine learning have evolved rapidly to accommodate the unique challenges of large language models. Traditional MLOps practices — model versioning, experiment tracking, automated retraining — remain relevant but are now supplemented by LLM-specific concerns: prompt management, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipelines, evaluation frameworks, and cost optimization for inference workloads.

Our data shows that organizations running LLM-based applications in production face significant infrastructure challenges: the average LLM-powered feature costs $18,000 per month in inference compute, and optimizing inference costs has become a full-time role at many organizations. Techniques like model caching, batched inference, and speculative decoding can reduce these costs by 50-70%, but require specialized engineering expertise.

6. Predictions for 2025-2026

Based on our analysis, we offer the following predictions for the cloud infrastructure landscape over the next 12-18 months:

  1. FinOps will become a board-level concern. As cloud spending exceeds $400B globally, CFOs and boards will demand real-time cost visibility and engineering-led optimization programs. Organizations that master FinOps will have a measurable competitive advantage in margins.
  2. Platform engineering teams will adopt AI-assisted development. AI coding assistants will be integrated directly into internal developer platforms, generating infrastructure-as-code, suggesting architectural patterns, and automating routine platform maintenance tasks.
  3. WebAssembly will emerge as a serious alternative to containers for specific workload classes. Its near-instant startup time, language-agnostic runtime, and strong sandboxing make it particularly attractive for edge computing and serverless functions.
  4. The "AI infrastructure stack" will consolidate. Today's fragmented landscape of MLOps, vector databases, model serving, and evaluation tools will consolidate into integrated platforms, much as the container ecosystem consolidated around Kubernetes.
  5. Sustainability will drive infrastructure decisions. Carbon-aware computing — scheduling workloads based on grid carbon intensity, selecting regions by renewable energy availability — will move from niche practice to mainstream requirement.

7. Methodology

This report is based on a combination of primary survey data and direct infrastructure analysis. Our survey was conducted between October and December 2024, collecting responses from 2,847 engineering leaders (VP of Engineering, Director of Infrastructure, Staff+ Engineers) across 42 countries. Respondents represent organizations ranging from 50-employee startups to Fortune 100 enterprises across 18 industry verticals.

Additionally, with explicit consent, we analyzed anonymized telemetry data from 15,247 production deployments across our research partners' infrastructure. This data was used to validate survey responses and provide quantitative backing for trends reported qualitatively. All individual data was aggregated and anonymized before analysis.

The margin of error for survey results at the 95% confidence interval is plus or minus 1.8 percentage points. Certain subgroup analyses (by company size, industry, or region) have wider margins of error, noted where applicable.
